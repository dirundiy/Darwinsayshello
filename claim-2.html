Claim Two

Descent From a Common Ancestor

The question

How do fossils and DNA sequencing support this?

Darwin's second claim states that different species can be traced back to shared ancestors. This doesn't mean that we came from modern apes living today. Instead, it means that humans and modern apes share an ancient ancestor that lived millions of years ago. Scientists use fossils and DNA sequencing to figure out how different organisms are connected on the family tree.

Evidence One

Fossil Record

Fossils show how organisms have changed over long periods of time. Scientists have discovered thousands of early human fossils and bones including skulls, teeth, and skeletons. When fossils from different time periods are compared, the scientists can see changes in features like brain size, face shape, and the ability to walk upright.

Reasoning

These fossils help connect older species to newer species and show that humans are part of a branching family tree (Smithsonian Human Origins Program).

Evidence Two

Similarities in DNA

Scientists can sequence and compare the DNA of different species. When two species have many similar DNA sequences, it suggests that they inherited DNA from a shared ancestor. Species that are more closely related usually have more DNA similarities than species that are distantly related.

Reasoning

These patterns let scientists create evolutionary family trees and understand how species are separated over time.

Evidence Three

Ancient DNA From Fossils

Scientists have recovered DNA from fossils, including fossils from Neanderthals. When Neanderthal DNA was compared to modern human DNA, researchers found out that Neanderthals and modern humans shared a common ancestor. The DNA also showed that the two groups sometimes reproduced with each other.

Reasoning

When ancient DNA and fossil evidence agree, they give more support for common ancestry (Smithsonian Human Origins Program).

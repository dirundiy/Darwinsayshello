Claim Five

Natural Selection

The question

How are climate change and environmental pressures shaping species now?

Darwin's fifth claim says that organisms with traits that help them survive in their environment are more likely to reproduce and pass those traits to their offspring. Over generations, those helpful traits become more common. Climate change and other environmental pressures can change which traits give organisms the best chance of surviving.

Evidence One

Light and Dark Peppered Moths

Peppered moths can be either light or dark. During the Industrial Revolution, pollution darkened many tree surfaces, making dark moths harder for birds to see. Light moths stood out more and were more likely to be eaten. After pollution decreased, lighter trees gave light moths better camouflage again. Experiments showed that birds were more likely to eat moths that did not blend into their surroundings.

Reasoning

This is natural selection because the environment affected which moth color had a better chance of surviving (Cook et al., 2012).

Evidence Two

Darwin's Finches

Peter and Rosemary Grant studied Galápagos finches for 30 years. They found that the size and shape of the birds' beaks changed as the environment and available food changed. During difficult conditions, finches with beaks that were better suited for the available seeds were more likely to survive and reproduce.

Reasoning

These changes were then seen in later generations (Grant & Grant, 2002).

Evidence Three

Green Anole Lizards

Scientists studied green anole lizards before and after an extreme winter storm. Many lizards died because of the cold, but the survivors were able to handle colder temperatures better. Researchers also found changes in the population's genes and physical traits.

Reasoning

The storm acted as an environmental pressure that favored lizards with better cold tolerance (Campbell-Staton et al., 2017).

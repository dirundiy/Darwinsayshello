Claim One

Nonconstancy of Species

The question

How do antibiotic-resistant bacteria evolve?

Nonconstancy of species means that species are not fixed and can change over generations. Antibiotic-resistant bacteria are a good modern day example of this claim. Some bacteria develop random mutations or get genes that allow them to survive an antibiotic. When the antibiotic kills the non-resistant bacteria, the resistant bacteria survive, reproduce, and also pass their resistance to future generations. Bacteria can also share resistance genes with other bacteria. Over time, the bacterial population becomes more resistant, which shows that living organisms can evolve and change.

Evidence One

Genetic Mutations

When bacteria reproduce, small random changes called mutations can happen in their DNA. Sometimes, a mutation helps a bacterium survive an antibiotic.

Reasoning

The resistant bacterium then reproduces and passes the helpful mutation to the next generation.

Evidence Two

Natural Selection

When an antibiotic is used, it kills the bacteria that are not resistant. However, resistant bacteria may survive and continue reproducing. After many generations, the population can contain mostly resistant bacteria.

Reasoning

This is natural selection because bacteria with the helpful resistance trait are more likely to survive and reproduce (CDC, 2026; Nimmana & Nguyen, 2026).

Evidence Three

Gene Sharing Between Bacteria

Bacteria can also pass antibiotic-resistance genes to each other through a process called horizontal gene transfer. Basically, one bacterium can share a small piece of DNA that helps another bacterium survive an antibiotic.

Reasoning

This can cause resistance to spread quickly through the whole group of bacteria (Nimmana & Nguyen, 2026).

Claim Four

Multiplication of Species

The question

How does speciation occur in different environments today?

Darwin's fourth claim says that one original species can eventually split into two or more new species. This process is called speciation. It can happen when groups live in different places, use different food sources, or start choosing different partners. Over time, the groups become so different that they rarely or no longer reproduce with each other (Barluenga et al., 2006).

Evidence One

Cichlid Fish in a Crater Lake

Scientists studied cichlid fish in Lake Apoyo, which is a volcanic crater lake in Nicaragua. They found that a new fish species developed from an older species in less than about 10,000 years. One group adapted to living and feeding in open water, while the original group stayed closer to the bottom.

Reasoning

The two groups developed different body shapes and became reproductively isolated, which means that they stopped regularly breeding with each other (Barluenga et al., 2006).

Evidence Two

Cichlid Fish Living at Different Water Depths

In Lake Victoria, different water depths have different amounts and colors of light. Some cichlid fish developed changes in their vision that helped them see better in their part of the lake.

Reasoning

Male fish also developed different colors, and female fish were more likely to choose males whose colors they could see best (Seehausen et al., 2008).

Evidence Three

Apple Maggot Flies Changing Host Plants

Apple maggot flies originally lived and reproduced on hawthorn fruit, but some began using apples instead. The apple flies and hawthorn flies became attracted to the smell of their own host fruit. Since the flies usually meet and mate near the fruit they use, the two groups started mating mostly within their own groups.

Reasoning

This created reproductive separation and shows how adapting to different food sources might lead to the formation of new species (Linn et al., 2004).

These examples support multiplication of species because they show how one population can separate into different groups. As each group adapts to its own environment, the groups can become reproductively isolated and eventually form separate species.

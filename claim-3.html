Claim Three

Gradual Evolution

The question

What modern examples, such as finch beak size or dog breeding, illustrate this?

Darwin's third claim says that evolution usually happens through small changes over many generations. A species does not suddenly become completely different. Instead, helpful traits slowly become more common in a population. After a long time, these small changes can create major differences.

Evidence One

Changes in Finch Beaks

Peter and Rosemary Grant studied Galápagos finches for 30 years. They found that the birds' body sizes and beak traits changed several times as environmental conditions and food supplies changed.

Reasoning

This shows that small changes can occur in a population across generations (Grant & Grant, 2002).

Evidence Two

Selective Breeding in Dogs

Humans have bred dogs for traits such as size, appearance, and behavior. Scientists compared the DNA of different dog breeds and found genetic patterns caused by artificial selection.

Reasoning

This shows how repeatedly choosing certain inherited traits can slowly create big differences between groups (Akey et al., 2010).

Evidence Three

Changes in Stickleback Fish

Scientists studied ocean stickleback fish that entered freshwater environments. They found that the frequencies of many genes changed in the new populations.

Reasoning

Many of the same genetic changes were also found in older freshwater populations, which shows how inherited changes can build up as organisms adapt to a new environment (Roberts Kingman et al., 2021).

Case Study Spotlight

How COVID-19 Variants Evolve

About COVID-19

COVID-19 is a disease caused by a virus called SARS-CoV-2. Since the virus first began spreading, it has continued to change and form new versions called variants. These changes happen because mutations build up in the virus's genetic material over time (CDC, 2025).

How COVID-19 Variants Evolve

When the virus enters a person's cells, it makes copies of itself. Small mistakes called mutations can happen during this process. Most mutations have little or no effect, but some may change how easily the virus spreads or how it reacts to vaccines, treatments, and the immune system (World Health Organization, 2026).

Variants with helpful mutations might spread to more people and become more common, while other variants may disappear. This is an example of natural selection because the versions of the virus that are better able to spread have a greater chance of making more copies of themselves (CDC, 2025).

Connection to Darwin's Claims

COVID-19 variants connect to a lot of Darwin's claims. They show nonconstancy of species because the virus population changes over time. They support common ancestry because each new variant comes from an earlier version of the virus. They also show gradual evolution because small mutations build up, and natural selection because some variants spread more successfully than others.

Nonconstancy of Species

  • The virus population changes over time.

Common Ancestry

  • Each new variant comes from an earlier version of the virus.

Gradual Evolution

  • Small mutations build up over time.

Natural Selection

  • Some variants spread more successfully than others.

Why This Is Important

Scientists use genomic sequencing to read the virus's genetic code and look for new variants. They compare samples to see how the variants are connected and which ones are spreading the most. This helps them track how the virus is changing and check whether tests, vaccines, and treatments still work well (CDC, 2025 and World Health Organization, 2026).
